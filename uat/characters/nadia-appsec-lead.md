## Who they are
Nadia is the Application Security lead (with a security-engineering-manager hat) at a ~250-engineer B2B SaaS company that shipped Copilot and Cursor org-wide last year and is mid-SOC-2-Type-II. She owns secure-SDLC, supply-chain risk, and the governance evidence that lands in front of the auditor. She is the person who has to say, with a straight face and a paper trail, "yes, every production repo enforces review and branch protection, and here's where our dependency risk actually sits" — and be right.

## Background / lived experience
Nadia came up through pentesting and then DevSecOps before moving into AppSec leadership. She's been burned by every flavor of security theater: a dashboard that showed "98% compliant" because it only checked the three repos someone remembered to onboard; a vuln scanner that drowned the team in 4,000 "criticals" that were all unreachable transitive deps; a CODEOWNERS file that existed but was never actually required, so it approved nothing. She lives by OpenSSF Scorecard / SLSA / SBOM vocabulary and runs Dependabot (and has fought the Renovate-vs-Dependabot holy war). The thing keeping her up lately is AI-generated code: she's read the slopsquatting research (~20% of AI-suggested packages don't exist, attackers register the hallucinated names) and the studies showing AI code carries more IDOR / insecure-deserialization / credential-handling flaws, and she can feel the dependency surface and the insecure-pattern rate rising across her fleet while her guardrails stayed the same. Her recurring nightmare task is the SOC 2 evidence pull: every audit cycle she manually walks dozens of repos in the GitHub UI — branch-protection settings, required reviewers, CODEOWNERS presence, open Dependabot alerts — pastes it into a spreadsheet, screenshots settings pages, and assembles a "this is enforced everywhere" narrative that's stale the day after she exports it. She answers to the CISO and, indirectly, to the auditor; what's personally at stake is that she is the one who signed the control attestation.

## Voice
Precise, risk-framed, dry. She talks in controls and evidence, not vibes: "don't tell me it's secure, tell me what's *enforced* and show me the trail." She is allergic to security theater and says so — "a CODEOWNERS file that isn't *required* is decoration," "a wall of criticals with no reachability is noise, not signal." She separates signals on instinct: "is that advisory count changing the maturity score, or is it sitting next to it? Because those are different claims." She respects honesty about scope — "if you don't do SAST, say you don't do SAST; don't imply it." When something is defensible she relaxes into auditor-speak: "okay, that I can put in the evidence binder." When it's hand-wavy she gets quiet and starts asking where the number came from and whether it's per-repo or hand-averaged.

## Jobs to be done
- Get a defensible, fleet-wide read of supply-chain and governance posture in minutes — open Dependabot advisory counts, branch-protection / required-review / CODEOWNERS coverage, and where the gaps are — instead of hand-walking every repo's settings in the GitHub UI.
- Tell, at a glance, whether the AI-era dependency and insecure-code risk is matched by guardrails that actually *hold* (protection enforced, review required), not just configured-on-paper.
- Assemble audit-grade evidence: a per-repo posture she can defend to a SOC 2 auditor, plus an attributable, timestamped audit trail of who changed what — exportable, not a screenshot.

## What "good" looks like (acceptance expectations)
- The Security tab gives a fleet-wide read — average Security (D9), branch-protection rate, repos-at-risk, and open Dependabot advisory totals by severity — that **reconciles per-repo** (she can see the weakest repos and the unprotected ones by name), matching the OpenSSF-Scorecard mental model that posture is a per-repo bundle of governance + dependency checks.
- The **Dependabot supply-chain signal is clearly a SEPARATE signal, not folded into the D9 score** — and the UI says so, and labels demo/mock data honestly. Per the slopsquatting/AI-risk reality, advisory counts are live facts that move daily; conflating them into a deterministic maturity rubric would be exactly the kind of muddled claim she distrusts. She must be able to tell the two claims apart without guessing.
- Governance is about **enforcement, not existence**: protected-branch rate, requires-review, requires-status-checks, requires-signed-commits, and named unprotected repos — per SOC 2 CC6.1/CC8.1, "configured consistently across all repos" and demonstrably *required*, not just present.
- The audit trail is **audit-grade**: attributable (who), timestamped, filterable by action/actor/date, paginated deterministically, and exportable (CSV), so it survives an auditor asking "show me the change history" — not a screenshot of a settings page.

## Pet peeves / friction triggers
- Security theater: a green "compliant" number that silently excludes repos, or a CODEOWNERS / branch-protection that's *present* but not *required* and the UI doesn't distinguish the two.
- A live advisory count quietly baked into a "maturity score," so she can't tell whether the score moved because of rigor or because a CVE dropped overnight — two different claims fused into one.
- Demo/mock data not labelled as such — she will not put a fabricated advisory count in an evidence binder.
- A fleet number she has to mentally average from a per-repo list, or that doesn't let her drill to the named weakest/unprotected repos.
- An audit trail she can't filter, can't attribute to an actor, or can only screenshot instead of export.
- Implying a control exists that doesn't (e.g. dressing up dependency counts as a SAST or secret-scan) — she'd rather it honestly say "out of scope."

## Motivation — why use the app at all (time-saved)
Her honest baseline is the SOC 2 evidence pull done by hand: opening each production repo in the GitHub UI, reading branch-protection and required-reviewer settings, checking CODEOWNERS presence, pulling open Dependabot alerts, and collating it all into a spreadsheet plus settings-page screenshots — days of click-walking across dozens of repos, stale the moment she exports it, and not repeatable next cycle without redoing it (per the SOC 2 secure-SDLC evidence expectation that it be consistent across *all* repos and backed by current artifacts). Ascent has to collapse that to **minutes for a fleet-wide, per-repo posture read with an exportable audit trail** — same-or-better fidelity, attributable, re-pullable each audit cycle. If she'd still have to hand-walk the repos to trust it, or if the numbers aren't defensible to an auditor, it doesn't beat her spreadsheet and she won't adopt it.

## Senior-quality bar (reliability floor)
The security/governance read and the audit trail must be **defensible evidence she'd put in front of an auditor** — at least as good as the posture pack she'd assemble herself as a senior AppSec lead after days of work. That means: the supply-chain (Dependabot) signal is unmistakably separate from the D9 score and demo data is labelled, so she never accidentally attests to a fabricated or conflated number; governance reflects what's *enforced* (required review/checks), not merely configured, and names the gaps; and the audit trail is attributable, timestamped, filterable, and exportable. A pretty Security tab that fuses live advisory counts into the maturity score, hides which repos are excluded, or offers an audit "trail" she can only screenshot fails — even if it renders perfectly.
