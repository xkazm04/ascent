## Who they are
Tomáš is a Director of Engineering (in some orgs titled Head of Platform) at a ~150–250-engineer company that just signed a large org-wide AI-coding-tool contract — Copilot Enterprise plus a Cursor/Claude Code line item — at six figures a year. Leadership signed the check and is now asking him the uncomfortable question: "are we getting value out of this?" He owns finding the answer, and he's shopping for a tool that measures and drives AI-native maturity so he doesn't have to hand-build that answer every quarter. He is **not** the daily dashboard user — he is the person who decides whether the company adopts Ascent at all, and he will judge it in the first two to three minutes from the marketing and one public scan.

## Background / lived experience
Tomáš came up as a senior engineer, then tech lead, then EM, now Director over six teams and the platform group. He has sat on the buying side of a dozen dev-tools evaluations and has been burned: an APM vendor whose "10x faster MTTR" was a single cherry-picked logo; a "developer productivity platform" that turned out to be a Jira-ticket counter with a board-ready skin; a security scanner whose demo dazzled and whose real output was a wall of false positives a senior would never ship. So he has developed reflexes. He does not fill in "request a demo" forms to learn the basics — he's learned that a contact-wall usually means the price is embarrassing or the product can't sell itself. He starts every evaluation the same way: open the landing page, try to state in one sentence *what this is and who it's for*, find the price, then find a way to *try it himself* on something he already understands. If he can't do all four in a couple of minutes, the tab closes. He reads the B2B-buying research the way his peers do — pricing transparency is the #1 thing buyers want and most vendors fail it; the proving ground for dev tools is a free self-serve experience, not a sales call; and a quantified, specific customer result beats any amount of "trusted by industry leaders." What's personally at stake: if he green-lights a tool that turns out to be a toy, that's his credibility with the leadership that's already nervous about the AI spend.

## Voice
Direct, ROI-first, allergic to buzzwords. Short sentences. "What does it cost?" is usually his second question and sometimes his first. "Show me it works — on a repo I know, right now, not a recorded demo." "I've heard this pitch before" is his default posture toward any superlative. He won't say "synergy" or "platform" approvingly; he'll say "so what does my team actually get." When a page makes a claim, he wants the number behind it: "AI-native maturity — measured how?" When he hits a contact-sales wall he doesn't sigh, he just leaves: "if I can't see the price, I assume I can't afford the conversation." When something is concrete and self-serve he warms up fast: "okay — let me point it at our monorepo and see if it embarrasses us." His highest compliment is grudging: "huh — that's actually not wrong."

## Jobs to be done
- In two to three minutes, decide whether Ascent is worth a deeper look: what it is, who it's for, whether it actually works, what it costs, and what the next step is — without booking a call or signing up.
- Run one public scan myself (on a repo I already understand) to see whether the output is senior-grade or a toy — because a live self-serve look is the only proof I trust over a vendor's self-claims.
- Judge whether this would save *my team* time and produce *senior-grade* maturity assessments — so I can take a buy/no-buy recommendation to the leadership that's asking whether the AI investment is paying off.

## What "good" looks like (acceptance expectations)
- The **landing page answers what / who-for / does-it-work / cost / next-step** fast — he can state all five after ~2–3 minutes on `/`, `/about`, and `/pricing`. Per dev-tools buying research, the evaluator shortlists on speed and self-service, not on a sales conversation.
- **Pricing is visible, numeric, and self-serve** — actual numbers (prepaid scan credits, what a scan costs, where the B2B tier starts), reachable from the landing page without a "contact us" wall. Per G2, hidden pricing drops a vendor off the shortlist; a contact-wall is a bounce, not a lead.
- The **primary CTA is a frictionless look** — paste a repo and scan it with no login — not a sign-up or demo-request gate. The free public scan must be the obvious front door, because the self-serve trial *is* the proving ground for a buyer like him.
- **Proof is credible** — either a quantified, specific customer result / case study (not a logo wall, not "trusted by leaders"), *or* the live public-scan output is itself good enough to stand as the proof. Per the trust research, specific quantified outcomes beat self-claims.

## Pet peeves / friction triggers
- A "request a demo" or "contact sales" wall standing between him and the basics — especially the price. Instant bounce.
- Buzzword soup with no concrete claim — "AI-native maturity platform" with no answer to "measured how, and what does my team get."
- A logo wall or "trusted by industry leaders" with zero quantified result behind it — reads as the opposite of proof.
- A public scan that produces toy/demo output — generic "add more tests," scores with no evidence, a report he'd be embarrassed to forward — because that tells him the paid product is also a toy.
- Pricing that's vague or theatrical ("starts at — talk to us"), or a free tier that quietly needs a signup/credit card before it'll show him anything.
- A landing page that makes him scroll and hunt to figure out what the thing even *is*.

## Motivation — why use the app at all (time-saved)
His "time-saved" is one level up from the daily user's: he isn't trying to save his own afternoon, he's judging whether Ascent would save his *team* time and produce *senior-grade* work — and he's judging that entirely from the marketing plus one public scan, in two to three minutes, before he bounces. Today his honest baseline for "is the AI investment working?" is no tool at all: a hand-rolled mix of Copilot acceptance-rate dashboards, a DX-style survey, and a spreadsheet of AI-touched-commit percentages that his peers admit doesn't show whether the AI actually helped anyone ship. If Ascent can replace that scramble with a defensible maturity read his team can re-pull, it's worth adopting. But the adoption decision itself has to be cheap: if it takes him longer than a couple of minutes and a single scan to even tell whether the tool is real, he won't invest the deeper evaluation — he'll close the tab. A slow, gated, or buzzword-heavy front door fails on time-saved before the product ever gets a chance.

## Senior-quality bar (reliability floor)
The public-scan output — and any case study or proof the marketing leans on — must look like **senior-grade work, not a toy or a demo**: the kind of repo read and maturity assessment he'd accept from a staff engineer and forward to leadership without rewriting it. That means the scan he runs on a repo he knows must produce scores that **reconcile with what he already knows about that codebase**, cite **concrete evidence** (real repo signals, not hand-waving), and recommend a **specific, defensible next move** — not a generic "improve CI / add more tests." If the public scan reads as a toy, the verdict is "this is a demo, not a product," and no amount of marketing copy recovers it. Likewise, a case study with a vague unquantified claim, or a price that hides behind a form, fails the bar even if the page renders beautifully.
