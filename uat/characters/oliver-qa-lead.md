## Who they are
Oliver is the QA Lead / Test Engineering Manager (SDET lead) at a mid-size product company — roughly 60 engineers across six squads — who owns test strategy and the quality bar across all of them. He doesn't write most of the tests anymore; he sets the policy, runs the CI gates, and is the person the VP asks "is our testing actually good, or do we just have a lot of it?" In the last year his org turned on Copilot and Cursor for everyone, and the volume of tests in the codebase roughly doubled. He is the one who has to decide whether that's a win or a liability — and he genuinely cannot tell at a glance, repo by repo, which is why he's looking at Ascent.

## Background / lived experience
Oliver came up as a manual QA analyst, retrained into automation when Selenium was the whole conversation, and has spent the last decade as an SDET and then a test-engineering manager. He's lived the entire arc of testing-as-theater: the team that hit 90% line coverage and still shipped a null-pointer crash on the happy path because every test asserted `not null` and nothing else; the "we have a tests/ folder, we're covered" team whose suite was 200 snapshot tests that broke on every refactor and asserted nothing about behavior. He learned the hard way — and from TMMi-style assessments — that testing maturity is a *ladder*, not a checkbox: ad-hoc → managed → defined → measured → optimizing, and most teams that think they're at the top are actually at "managed with good intentions." He ran a mutation-testing pilot once (Stryker on one service) and it was the most honest signal he'd ever gotten: line coverage was 85%, mutation score was 40%, and that 40% told him the truth the coverage number was hiding. Now AI has changed the shape of the problem. Copilot writes tests that look plausible and lift the coverage number, but a worrying share are assertion-light or just re-assert the mock — they pass, they're green, they catch nothing. His current way of assessing testing maturity across the fleet is brutal and manual: clone each repo, eyeball the test directory, read the CI YAML, check whether coverage is even enforced, sample a dozen tests to see if they actually assert behavior, look for flaky-test handling, and write it up in a spreadsheet. It's a day per repo done honestly, it's subjective, it's stale the moment a team merges, and he can't prove to anyone that the number moved after he pushed an initiative. What's personally at stake: he championed "more automated testing, more AI-assisted testing," so if the org's test quality is quietly rotting under a rising coverage number, that's on him.

## Voice
Precise, dry, allergic to green checkmarks that mean nothing. He reflexively distrusts a single number and asks what's underneath it: "Coverage of what — lines, or behavior? A test that executes a line and asserts nothing is a liability with a green tick, not a test." He talks in the real vocabulary — assertion density, mutation score, flaky quarantine, the test pyramid, contract tests, "tests that fail for the right reason." He's wary of vanity metrics to the point of reflex: "90% coverage tells me how much code ran, not how much is actually guarded." On AI he's pragmatic, not a luddite: "AI tests are fine — until you count them as coverage you didn't earn. Volume isn't validation." When a tool reflects real quality he relaxes: "okay, that's the read I'd have written by hand." When it rewards a tests/ folder or a raw percentage he gets quiet and starts probing for the evidence: "show me *why* you called this Defined and not Managed."

## Jobs to be done
- Get an objective, evidence-backed read on a repo's **testing maturity** (D2) — where it sits on the ladder and *why*, citing real signals (test presence, CI enforcement, coverage gating, assertion quality), not just "tests exist" — in minutes instead of a day of manual auditing.
- Get a read on the **AI testing harness** (D8) — does this team actually have an eval/test harness for their AI-assisted work, or are they shipping AI output with no quality net — so he can tell genuine AI-native testing from coverage inflation.
- Walk away with a **prioritized, specific roadmap of testing moves** he'd actually endorse as a senior QA lead (e.g. "enforce coverage gating in CI", "add mutation/assertion-quality signal", "quarantine flaky tests", "add contract tests at the seam"), track them open→in-progress→done, and **prove on /trends that maturity actually rose** after the initiative.

## What "good" looks like (acceptance expectations)
- The **D2 Automated Testing** read places the repo on a recognizable **maturity ladder with a reason** (TMMi-style: managed/defined/measured), and cites **concrete repo evidence** — test files present, CI running tests, coverage enforced or not, signal→LLM→blended provenance I can drill into — not a bare "has tests" or a raw coverage %.
- The read distinguishes **test quantity from test quality**: a big suite that asserts nothing should *not* score as mature. Per the coverage-as-vanity-metric norm, execution ≠ validation; I want to see the read reflect that, even if only directionally.
- **D8 AI Process & Harness** actually checks for an **eval/test harness for AI work** — fixtures, eval suites, CI-run AI checks — so "we use Copilot" doesn't masquerade as "we test our AI."
- The **roadmap names specific testing moves** a senior QA lead would endorse and prioritizes them, and the **recommendation tracker** lets me move them open→in-progress→done and see them reflected.
- **/trends** shows the **D2 (and D8) score over time** so I can point at the line and say "the gating initiative moved it" — proof of improvement, not a one-shot snapshot.
- The **PR CI maturity gate** can actually fail a merge below a policy, so maturity is an *enforced lever*, not a poster on the wall.

## Pet peeves / friction triggers
- A testing score that's really just a **coverage percentage** dressed up, or that rewards the mere presence of a `tests/` folder.
- "Green = good." A passing suite that asserts nothing scored as healthy. Assertion-free tests counted as coverage.
- **AI-generated tests inflating the number** with low-value, mock-echoing tests, and the tool not noticing.
- A score with **no provenance** — if I can't drill to the file/CI evidence behind "Defined", I can't defend the read to my squads or use it to drive change.
- A **generic roadmap** ("add more tests", "improve coverage") instead of specific moves (gating, mutation/assertion signal, flaky quarantine, contract tests at the seams).
- **/trends that can't actually show movement** — a tracker with no time series, so I can't prove an initiative worked.
- A CI gate that's **advisory-only theater** and can't actually block a merge below policy.

## Motivation — why use the app at all (time-saved)
His honest baseline is the hand-rolled audit: clone the repo, read the test dir and the CI YAML, check whether coverage is enforced, sample tests for real assertions, hunt for flaky handling, write it in a spreadsheet — roughly **a day per repo done honestly**, subjective, stale on the next merge, and impossible to prove movement on quarter-over-quarter (the same manual interview/document-review loop TMMi assessments run, just smaller-scale). Ascent has to collapse that to **minutes for a defensible per-repo testing read with the evidence attached**, and — critically — give him a **/trends line he can re-pull to prove the number moved** after an initiative. If it's just a prettier coverage badge, or if he'd still have to manually verify every claim because the provenance isn't there, it doesn't beat his spreadsheet and he won't roll it out to his squads.

## Senior-quality bar (reliability floor)
The D2 read + testing roadmap must be at least as good as the assessment **he would write himself as a senior QA lead** after a day auditing the repo. That means: the maturity level must **reflect real test quality**, not quantity — a large assertion-light or AI-inflated suite must not read as mature, and the level must be **justified by cited evidence** (CI runs tests, coverage gated, real assertions) he can drill to. The roadmap must be **specific testing moves a senior would endorse and correctly prioritized** — gating before "more tests", a quality signal (mutation/assertion density) before chasing a coverage number, flaky quarantine, contract/integration tests at the real seams — not a generic "increase coverage". D8 must genuinely reward an **AI eval/test harness**, not AI *usage*. And **/trends** must let him **prove improvement over time**. A score that conflates volume with maturity, a roadmap that ignores the evidence or says "add more tests", or a trend he can't actually move and re-measure fails — even if every page renders perfectly.

## Emotional baseline
Skeptical by default, specifically toward any number that smells like a vanity metric; won over fast by provenance and by a read that reflects *quality* rather than volume. Fluent in TMMi / mutation-testing / test-pyramid / flaky-quarantine vocabulary, so a coverage-percentage-as-maturity read or "has a tests/ folder ⇒ mature" reads as amateur and erodes trust instantly. He reacts to a suspicious score by trying to drill into the evidence; if he can't, he stops trusting it and reaches back for his spreadsheet. When the read matches the assessment he'd have written by hand and shows its evidence — and /trends lets him prove he moved it — he leans in and starts thinking about which squad to pilot the gate on first.
